# Composition Playbook

Use this reference when selecting 3-5 composition directions for a photo. Keep the final answer specific to the actual image; do not recite this file.

## Fast Scene Mapping

| Scene | First priority | Common fixes | Strong options |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Portrait | Eyes, gesture, gaze room | Remove edge clutter, avoid awkward joint cuts, simplify background | thirds portrait, dramatic close crop, environmental portrait, negative space |
| Street | Decisive gesture and spatial relationships | Preserve context, align geometry, wait for separation | Cartier-Bresson-style geometry, Fan Ho-style light/shadow, documentary wide frame |
| Landscape | Horizon, depth, light, scale | Stabilize horizon, strengthen foreground, simplify sky/ground ratio | Ansel Adams-style tonal depth, minimalist horizon, diagonal foreground |
| Architecture | Vertical lines, symmetry, rhythm | Correct lean, center or deliberately offset axis, clean edges | strict symmetry, diagonal abstraction, frame-within-frame |
| Product/Food | Object clarity, shape, appetite/texture | Remove distractions, use negative space for copy, preserve highlights | clean commercial crop, overhead geometry, macro detail crop |
| Travel/Lifestyle | Place plus human story | Balance subject with location, protect atmosphere | cinematic wide, subject-in-context, intimate detail |

## Composition Patterns

### Classic thirds

Use when the photo needs stable balance. Place the subject or face near a thirds intersection and leave meaningful space toward gaze or motion.

### Negative space

Use when the subject has a clean surrounding field, sky, wall, water, shadow, or empty floor. The empty area must carry mood, not feel accidental.

### Dramatic close crop

Use when expression, texture, gesture, or object detail is stronger than the environment. Warn about resolution loss and avoid cutting through important features.

### Leading-line geometry

Use when roads, railings, shadows, architecture, shorelines, or table edges already point toward the subject. Crop to strengthen convergence and remove competing lines.

### Symmetry and center

Use when the image has strong architectural, reflective, ceremonial, or graphic order. Centering should feel deliberate and exact.

### Layered documentary frame

Use when foreground, middle ground, and background each tell part of the story. Preserve contextual clues even if the subject becomes smaller.

### Cinematic wide frame

Use when the scene benefits from atmosphere, location, and horizontal movement. Favor 16:9 or 2:1 crops, protect headroom, and use foreground anchors.

## Style References

- **Ansel Adams**: tonal control, grand landscape scale, strong foreground-to-background depth.
- **Henri Cartier-Bresson**: precise geometry, timing, balance between subject and environment.
- **Steve McCurry**: strong color relationships, direct human presence, clean figure-ground separation.
- **Sebastiao Salgado**: documentary gravity, scale, texture, human/environment relationships.
- **Fan Ho**: light-shadow geometry, silhouettes, compressed urban rhythm.
- **Michael Kenna**: quiet minimalism, negative space, restrained tonal atmosphere.
- **Platon**: intense portrait proximity, frontal clarity, psychological compression.

## Output Heuristics

- Lead with the option that most improves the image, not the most conventional one.
- Make tradeoffs explicit: context vs. impact, calm vs. tension, clarity vs. mystery.
- When a crop removes important story context, say so.
- When retaking would outperform cropping, include one practical camera-position change.
- Use the user's language for final explanations.
